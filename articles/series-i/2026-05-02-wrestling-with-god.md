# Wrestling With God

## Opening Scripture

“Then Jacob was left alone; and a Man wrestled with him until the breaking of day.” — Genesis 32:24

---

## Body

After the question of salvation comes the confrontation of the soul.

Wrestling with God is not rebellion when the wrestling leads to surrender. It is the moment where the old self runs out of places to hide. It is where faith stops being inherited language and becomes personal encounter.

Jacob was left alone.

That is where the real wrestling begins.

Not in the crowd.

Not in performance.

Not in public religion.

Alone.

There comes a point when a person has to stand before God without excuses, without audience, without armor, and without the old story. That is where the Lord exposes what has been running the life beneath the surface.

Fear.

Control.

Pride.

Ambition.

Pain.

Self-protection.

The desire to be blessed without being changed.

Jacob wanted blessing, but God wanted transformation. Jacob wanted forward movement, but God touched the place of strength. Jacob left that encounter with a new name, but he also left with a limp.

That is the mystery of true formation.

God does not merely bless the old self.

He breaks its authority.

This sermon belongs second because no person moves cleanly from salvation into surrender without conflict. The flesh resists. The ego negotiates. The old identity fights to survive. The soul wants God’s promise but still fears God’s process.

Wrestling with God is the collision between who you were and who God is calling you to become.

It is not pretty.

It is not comfortable.

But it is holy.

This is the place where a person realizes that God is not trying to decorate the old life. He is calling forth a new one. The old name cannot carry the new calling. The old way cannot sustain the promise. The old strength cannot become the foundation of the Fifth Gospel.

That is why the wrestling matters.

Before Mirror, the soul must become honest enough to look.

Before Water, the soul must become humble enough to be washed.

Before Fire, the soul must become surrendered enough to be refined.

Before Abide in Christ, the soul must stop pretending it can live apart from Him.

Jacob wrestled until the breaking of day.

That phrase matters.

Because night does not last forever, but neither does transformation happen instantly. Sometimes the wrestling continues until something in you finally breaks. Not to destroy you, but to deliver you from the false version of strength you trusted more than God.

The blessing came with a wound.

The new name came with a limp.

The future came after surrender.

That is the pattern.

Many people want God to bless their plans. But wrestling with God reveals that the plan itself may need to die. Many people want God to strengthen their identity. But God may be giving them a new one. Many people want victory without exposure, cleansing without repentance, and fire without surrender.

But the Gospel does not work that way.

You cannot become the Fifth Gospel while still hiding from the God who reads every page.

Wrestling with God is the moment where the private life comes under divine examination. It is where prayer becomes confrontation. It is where the soul stops asking God to approve the old life and starts asking God to form the new one.

This is not weakness.

This is formation.

The world teaches people to avoid struggle. But Scripture shows that some struggles are sacred. Some nights are necessary. Some wounds are gifts because they remind the believer never to walk in self-rule again.

Jacob did not leave the same.

That is the point.

If an encounter with God leaves everything unchanged, we must ask whether we encountered God or only an idea of God.

True encounter changes the walk.

True encounter changes the name.

True encounter changes the appetite.

True encounter changes the future.

Series I continues here because surrender is not theoretical. It is forged through confrontation. The believer must stop performing faith and begin yielding the life.

Wrestling with God is the death of spiritual pretending.

It is where the old self says, “I will not let go unless You bless me.”

And God answers by changing the one who asks.

This is the second article in Surrender to Fire.

The question has been asked:

Did you have salvation to begin with?

Now the soul is brought into the night.

Alone with God.

Face to face with truth.

Until the blessing comes.

Until the name changes.

Until the old walk is broken.

Until the new life begins.
